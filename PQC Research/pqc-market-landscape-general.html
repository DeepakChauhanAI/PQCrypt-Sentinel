Market Dynamics — What's Actually Happening
DimensionCurrent State (May 2026)Implication for a New Entrant
Market Phase Crossing from "awareness" to "early adoption." NIST 2030 deadline is real enough that CISOs are now budgeting for it. EU Commission mandated full cryptographic inventory by end of 2026 for all member state organisations. US federal agencies under OMB M-23-02. The buyer is ready. The question is whether your product is easy enough to deploy and cheap enough to justify vs. building internally.
Consolidation Pace Rapid M&A — Keyfactor acquired two companies (InfoSec Global + CipherInsights) in 2025 alone. CyberArk acquired Venafi 2023. Palo Alto entered Jan 2026. The large players are assembling through acquisition, not building. Window to establish product identity and customer base is 2025–2027. After that, the market likely has 3–4 dominant platforms. Independent niches survive only with strong vertical lock-in.
Pricing Opacity Universal "contact sales." No vendor publishes pricing. Enterprise buyers overpay because they lack benchmarks. First-year professional services (8–12 weeks) often exceeds first-year license cost. Transparent pricing and a fast deployment path (days, not months) is a genuine enterprise differentiator — not just a nice-to-have.
Mid-Market Gap The $10K–$75K/yr segment is completely unserved. Open source requires too much engineering. Commercial tools start at $50K and quickly exceed $200K. Mid-size enterprises (500–5000 employees) have no good option. A product that genuinely deploys in a day and is priced at $15K–$50K/yr per organisation would own the mid-market with no competition.
Deployment Friction Every commercial product requires 8–12 weeks of professional services implementation. Even Keyfactor (the fastest) requires infrastructure setup. No commercial product has a "docker-compose up and scan" path. First-scan-in-under-an-hour is a product design constraint worth building around from day one.
PQC Traffic Reality As of end-2025, over 52% of TLS 1.3 traffic already uses PQC (driven by Chrome, Firefox, Cloudflare). This means passive network scanners will see hybrid PQC traffic everywhere — the detection problem is now also about classifying what's already there, not just what's missing. Scanners need to distinguish "has PQC but also allows downgrade" from "fully PQC-only." This nuance is absent from all open-source tools and shallow in most commercial ones.
04 — Gap Analysis
What the General Market Doesn't Cover

Three gap types: Total (nobody does it), Partial (done inadequately), Commercial (done but inaccessible by price/model).

🔐
HSM & Key Management Total Gap
  • PKCS#11 full key hierarchy enumeration (master key → zone key → session key) — mentioned in CycloneDX CBOM spec but implemented by nobody in their scanner.
  • HSM firmware PQC readiness matrix — no tool cross-references Thales Luna/Entrust firmware version against published PQC support dates.
  • KMIP-based KMS scanning — only AQtive Guard has partial support. Thales CipherTrust, IBM SKLM, HashiCorp Vault all expose KMIP but nobody scans it.
  • Database TDE algorithm scan (Oracle, MSSQL) — only QryptoCyber has this; every other tool misses encryption at rest in databases entirely.
  • Offline root CA assessment — the highest-priority step in any migration and completely ignored by all tools.
⚡
Runtime vs Static Analysis Total Gap
  • Every SAST tool (IBM QSE, QRAMM CryptoScan, Keyfactor AgileSec) finds algorithms statically present in code. None verify which are actually executed at runtime in production.
  • Only Binarly+QuSecure does reachability analysis — and they focus on firmware/binaries, not general enterprise applications.
  • False positive problem: SAST tools flag unused legacy code paths that aren't actually called. No tool currently filters by runtime reachability to reduce noise.
  • This gap means enterprises generate massive finding lists that security teams can't prioritise — everything looks equal when in reality only a subset is actively exploitable.
📊
Risk Scoring Depth Partial Gap
  • All open-source tools output raw findings with zero prioritisation. A CISO cannot hand the output of pqcscan or QRAMM CryptoScan to their team and get a prioritised work queue.
  • Commercial tools score risk but typically on 1–2 dimensions (algorithm + exposure). None implement Mosca's Theorem as a visible, interactive per-asset calculation.
  • HNDL risk (how many years of quantum-vulnerable exposure does this data face) is mentioned in vendor marketing but not surfaced as a calculated metric in any dashboard.
  • No tool connects data sensitivity classification (long-lived financial data vs short-lived session tokens) to remediation priority in an automated way.
📦
Deployment Model Total Gap
  • No commercial product deploys in a single docker-compose command with zero external dependencies. Every platform requires 8–12 weeks of professional services.
  • Open source tools require engineering effort to assemble — no one has packaged pqcscan + QRAMM + IBM QSE + risk engine + dashboard into one deployable unit.
  • The "first scan within one hour of deciding to try the product" use case is completely unserved in the commercial market.
  • Air-gap deployment (no internet access, no license server phone-home) is only genuinely supported by open source tools — commercial products all require connectivity.
💰
Mid-Market Pricing Commercial Gap
  • Organisations with 500–5000 employees (the global mid-market) have no viable commercial option. AppViewX starts at ~$50K but is SaaS-only. Keyfactor starts at ~$75K with an 8-week implementation. Open source requires too much engineering.
  • The $15K–$50K/yr price band for a self-hosted, full-coverage PQC scanner is completely empty. This is the natural price point for a 3-person security team at a mid-size enterprise.
  • Managed service providers (MSPs) serving SMEs have no white-label PQC scanning product to resell. An entire channel is waiting.
🔄
Remediation Workflow Partial Gap
  • Every tool stops at "here is what is vulnerable." Only QryptoCyber and ISARA Advance attempt sequenced remediation roadmaps — and neither integrates with ticketing systems (Jira, ServiceNow) out of the box.
  • No tool tracks migration progress over time with a % complete metric toward 2030/2035. Banks, enterprises, and auditors need evidence of progress — not just point-in-time snapshots.
  • Vendor PQC readiness assessment (is my OpenSSL version PQC-capable? Is my cloud provider's KMS ready?) is completely absent from all tools.
  • Crypto agility recommendations — how to refactor the code to decouple algorithm choice from implementation — are absent from all tools today.
05 — Verdict & Opportunity
Where to Play & What to Build

Synthesis into concrete product positioning and build decisions.

Competitive Verdicts
01
Assemble open source as your scanner engine
pqcscan + QRAMM + IBM QSE
Don't rebuild TLS/SSH scanning, SAST, or CBOM generation from scratch. These are solved problems with battle-tested open-source implementations. pqcscan for TLS/SSH PQC detection, QRAMM CryptoScan for SAST, ssh-audit for SSH config, IBM QSE for CBOM generation. Your value is the orchestration layer, risk engine, and deployment packaging on top.
02
Your primary moat is deployment simplicity + mid-market price
The gap nobody is filling
SandboxAQ and Keyfactor are winning on depth. Palo Alto is winning on distribution. The completely unoccupied position is: a full-coverage PQC scanner that deploys in one command, produces a risk-scored CBOM dashboard within an hour, and is priced for organisations that cannot justify $75K–$500K/yr. That product does not exist.
03
CryptoNext COMPASS is your closest architectural reference
Study the architecture, not the product
Open discovery layer (free) + passive probe (commercial) + analytics aggregation (commercial) is the cleanest architecture in the market. Their COMPASS Discovery layer is open source — read the code. Their weakness is EU-only distribution, high PS cost, and no mid-market path. Copy the architecture, fix the business model.

Strategic Conclusion — General Market The general enterprise PQC scanning market has two inhabited zones: free open-source tools with no integration and no risk scoring, and $200K–$2M enterprise platforms built for Fortune 500 and government. The mid-market ($15K–$75K, self-hosted, sub-hour deployment) is completely unoccupied and growing as NIST 2030 deadlines become real. A product that assembles the best open-source components into a deployable, risk-scored platform — priced for mid-market — has no direct competition today and a 2–3 year window before the large platforms build downmarket.
